An open inquiry · 2026

Measure the human, not just the model.

As AI does more of the work, what happens to the judgment of the person directing it, and can you even see it changing? An open inquiry into the gap, the evidence, and what I found when I measured my own.

Read the research document Skim the inquiry

The problem

We measure the machine obsessively. We don't measure what it's doing to us.

Two years of work has gone into knowing whether the model is good: evals, observability, agent memory. The human sitting next to it has nothing equivalent. You can audit an agent's every step. You cannot audit your own.

The evidence

It isn't speculative. It's measured, independently, and accelerating.

Across separate studies the same shape appears: AI makes the work easier and the human less critical, and the loss outlasts the tool.

N=758

Why now · 2026

The work has shifted from process to outcome, and the judgment moments are vanishing.

AI is becoming more relational, persistent, and accurate about you specifically, each one harder to push back on. The human's contact with the work narrows to three seams: the brief, the redirect, the sign-off. The people closest to it said the same thing in a single week.

I ran it on myself

I check the AI's work about one time in four.

Before deciding anyone else has this problem, I pointed the instrument at my own work: more than two hundred of my real coding sessions and the nine-hundred-odd messages I sent inside them. I wanted one thing — to see what I actually do when the machine hands me something finished.

I verify it's right · ~1 in 4 I steer the surface · ~1 in 4 I hand over the next task · ~1 in 3 I wave it through · ~1 in 7

Four moves show up. I verify it's right about one time in four. I steer how it looks about as often. A third of the time I'm just handing over the next task. And roughly one time in seven I wave it through without really reading it.

The shape of my own data is uncomfortable and specific. I'm good at steering the surface — fixing the voice, redirecting the approach — and weak at checking the substance. I catch what's wrong with how the AI writes. I coast on whether what it says is true.

I built the tool to find this, on my own work, half-expecting to come out looking fine. It caught me anyway, and that's the uncomfortable part: the awareness was already there. It wasn't enough.

Call it an estimate; measure it a few ways and it shifts a point or two. The shape doesn't move.

A potential approach

What a fix could look like: quiet, local, pointed at you.

Not a dashboard that nags, and not a score. A thin instrument that records the decisions behind your work and shows you the drift, the way a training log shows a runner their pace.

01

Capture, locally

A hook reads each session on your machine when it ends. Nothing identifiable leaves your device.

02

Reflect, privately

It records what you steered, what you checked, and what you let go. No score, no grade.

03

See the drift

Over time the mix and its trend become visible, at the one moment the loop can't watch itself: sign-off.

Runs and stays on your machine No score, ever No cross-user comparison Open source · MIT

A personal tool helps the person who runs it. On its own it won't save the population. That has always taken a curriculum, a platform, or a professional standard. This is an inquiry, not a verdict.

Open questions

What's still unknown.

  1. 01

    What's the right unit to watch? A single turn, a whole session, the irreversible commands, or the moment you accept a long run you never watched.

  2. 02

    How much checking is silent? Reading a diff and saying nothing is real scrutiny that no transcript can see.

  3. 03

    Does the gap compound over months, or do people correct once they can see it? Answering that needs far more history.

  4. 04

    Does showing someone their own mix change how they work, or just hand them a number to game?

  5. 05

    Is the right altitude the individual, the team's curriculum, or the platform itself? And how do you surface it without becoming the tool people switch off on day one?
