Measure the human, not just the model.

As AI does more of the work, what happens to the judgment of the person directing it — and can you even see it changing? An open inquiry: the problem, the research, and a potential approach.

Read the inquiry The full argument

The problem

We measure the machine obsessively. We don't measure what it's doing to us.

The last 18 months poured capital into knowing whether the model is good — evals, observability, agent memory. The human sitting next to it has nothing equivalent. You can audit an agent's every decision. You cannot audit your own.

Why now · 2026

The work has shifted from process to outcome — and the judgment moments are vanishing.

AI is becoming more relational, persistent, and accurate about you specifically — each harder to push back on. The human's contact with the work collapses to three seams: the brief, the redirect, the sign-off. The people closest to it described the same shift in a single week.

The research

It isn't speculative. It's measured, independently, and it's accelerating.

Across separate studies the same shape appears: AI makes the work easier and the human less critical — and the loss persists after the AI is gone.

A potential approach

What a fix could look like — quiet, private, and pointed at you.

Not a dashboard that nags. Not a score. A thin instrument that records the decisions behind your work and shows you the trend — the way a training log works for a runner. Here's the shape I'm testing.

01

Capture, locally

A Claude Code hook reads each session on your machine when it ends. Nothing identifiable leaves your device.

02

Reflect, privately

It writes a plain digest — what you steered, what you challenged, what you waved through. No score, no grade.

03

See the trend

Over time, the rate you stop owning the hard calls becomes visible — before output quality ever dips.

Runs and stays on your machine No score, ever No cross-user comparison Open source · MIT
