01 THE PROBLEM

Output isn't judgment.

You commit code. You close tickets. You ship features. Your output has never been higher.

But nothing measures whether the human behind the AI is improving, plateauing, or quietly atrophying. And there's a trap inside the speed: the more polished the output looks, the less you examine it. You stop questioning, stop fact-checking, stop pushing back.

This isn't about using AI less. It's about knowing whether you're still thinking while you use it.

03 THE TRAJECTORY

Atrophy doesn't announce itself.

It shows up first at the moments that carry the most weight — the irreversible ones. Most builders have turned permission prompts off, so today the highest-stakes actions (force-push, prod migration, DROP, merge to main) run with less scrutiny than trivial ones.

A dry run over real sessions made it measurable:

0one-way doors / day · 0.45% of commands
~6/8were rubber stamps: “go for it”, “ship it”

The rate at which you leave the justification blank is the first signal — the wanting-to-own fading, before output quality ever dips. Not a score. A read.

04 WHERE WE ARE · 2026

The work has shifted from process to outcome.

With this year's frontier models, the human's contact with the work collapses to three seams: the brief, the mid-flight redirect, and the sign-off. Fewer judgment moments, each carrying more weight, none of them instrumented. The people closest to it are saying it out loud.

05 THE INSTRUMENT

Crux makes the judgment layer visible.

A local-first instrument, and a small open inquiry, into the human side of AI collaboration. A Claude Code hook reads each session and writes a digest: what you steered, what you let slide, the patterns over time. Zero commands. No score. A mirror for you, not a certificate for someone else.
